> **Why "cut your losses" can be the losing move**
>
> Every trading book preaches the stop-loss: set a trailing stop, "cut your losers, ride your winners," stay disciplined. It feels smart. For a lot of strategies, it quietly bleeds money — and the reason is worth understanding, because it changed how we built ours.
>
> A stop sells you out based on price movement. So the real question is: at your holding horizon, does a price drop carry information — or is it just noise?
>
> - Over short horizons, most dips are noise. A strong stock routinely falls 8–15% for a few days and snaps right back — normal volatility, not a verdict. A tight stop yanks you out at the bottom of that random dip, locks in the loss, and the stock recovers without you. Death by a thousand whipsaws.
> - Over long horizons, a big drop is a signal. Hold for months and a deep drawdown genuinely means the trend has broken. There, a stop is exactly right.
>
> This is precisely why RigaCap's core strategy holds positions for months and uses a deliberately wide trailing stop. The width isn't laziness — it's the point. It ignores everyday noise and only reacts when a real reversal arrives. A tighter, "smarter"-sounding stop would whipsaw us out of every healthy pullback and cost us the biggest winners.
>
> The lesson: the most sophisticated-sounding exit isn't universally the best one. The right exit matches the horizon — the line between discipline and self-sabotage. We didn't take that on faith; we tested it on decades of data, survivorship-free. The answer surprised us too.
