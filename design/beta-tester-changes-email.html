Hi there,

I want to be straight with you about a couple of changes I just made to RigaCap — including one that's going to sound backwards at first. You're here early, so you get the honest version.

First, the uncomfortable part. When I went back and stress-tested our strategy on cleaner, more honest data, I found our old numbers had been flattering us. They quietly left out companies that went bankrupt, and a couple of data quirks had inflated the returns. So I rebuilt the whole thing from scratch — and revised our numbers down. Then I went further: I extended the test back through twenty-one years, so it had to survive the 2008 financial crisis, the COVID crash, and the 2022 bear before I'd believe it. I'd rather show you a smaller number I can stand behind than a big one I can't.

Now the part that sounds backwards. I also deliberately changed the strategy to chase less return — and more safety. On paper that looks like a downgrade. Let me explain why it's actually the opposite, because this is the most important thing I'll tell you all year.

There's a number that matters far more than the headline return, and almost nobody talks about it: the drawdown. It's simply how far your account falls from its highest point before it recovers.

Over those twenty-one years, raw momentum — the flashy version of this strategy — fell 57% from its peak. The S&P 500 lost more than half its value twice. In plain terms: you'd watch a $100,000 account become $45,000. And here's the problem — almost nobody can sit through that. People panic and sell at the very bottom, locking in the loss… right before the recovery they'd been waiting for. So those "high returns" only ever existed on paper, for a robot that never felt fear.

RigaCap's worst drop across the same twenty-one years — 2008 included — was about 19%. A 19% dip is something you can actually stomach, and hold through. In fact, we ran the numbers on what a normal human collects from each strategy if they bail whenever they're down 25%: the raw-momentum investor ends up with somewhere between $206k and $1M depending entirely on their nerve, after panic-selling six to ten times. RigaCap's path never once handed them a reason to panic.

Because the best strategy isn't the one with the biggest number on a chart. It's the one you can actually stay invested in. An 8% return you keep beats a 19% return you bail on — every single time. (And lately it's been far better than 8%: the last two years ran +32% annualized in our held-out test — but I want you anchored on the conservative number, not the shiny one.)

It's a bit like a car. One that does 200 mph but terrifies you past 60 is worse than one that does 120 you'll actually floor. The returns that reach your account are only the ones you can hold through.

One last bit of honesty. These are still backtested results — a rigorous simulation, not a live track record. (Even the data has a footnote: before 2016 it can't include every company that later disappeared, and we say so right on the methodology page.) But here's the news: the rebuilt strategy went live tonight, with a fresh $100,000 model book starting from zero. The live clock is running as you read this, and I'll show you the real results as they come in. Good or bad. That's the deal.

One more thing — go watch the chart on the track-record page. It races $100,000 through all twenty-one years, three crashes and all, and then shows what happens when a real human panic-sells along the way. It's the clearest explanation of why I built this that I've ever managed.

Thank you for being here early. You're not just testing a product — you're keeping me honest. That's worth more than you know.

— Erik
